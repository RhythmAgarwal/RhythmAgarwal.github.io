LinkedIn Design Exercise

Designing for barriers in sharing




Problem

LinkedIn enables professionals to be more productive and successful by helping them stay informed and build meaningful relationships. A productive content ecosystem relies on a healthy balance of content contributors and consumers that share and learn knowledge about their industries, careers and professional interests.

For many members, however, it feels scary or risky to contribute to the platform since their actions are tied to their professional identities.




RESEARCH

Research Questions

To understand the problem better I wanted answers to a few questions -

• Why are users not sharing on LinkedIn?
• What are the barriers faced by users when sharing on LinkedIn?
• Why motivates users to share on their professional network?
• What are their fears?

Finding Answers

• To get answers to the above questions I talked to 9 friends in my network who are on LinkedIn as professionals.
• To validate what I was hearing and to to get the most prominent pain points and motivations I sent out a survey (which received 42 responses). This helped me scope down.
• I also read a lot on LinkedIn Help about sharing and articles by a few LinkedIn employees on sharing, their stories and tips.




KEY FINDINGS

Putting together what I had heard in casual interviews and data from the survey, I came up with 3 major barriers that I will try to solve during the solution phase of the exercise. I also outlined the motivations found in research so that I can design solutions that help novice contributors achieve their goals
by removing the barriers.

Barriers

• I am not sure what I should be posting (~43% of the surveyed)
There is a fear of wasting network’s time and coming across as unprofessional because novice contributors are not aware of their options and they don’t know what is acceptable and appropriate to share with their network.

• I don’t have an incentive to share (~47% of the surveyed)
Novice contributors lack the extrinsic motivation to share on the network

• I am worried that my content is not good enough (~33% of the surveyed)
There is a fear of judgement on bad content because novice contributors lack the confidence and assurance



Motivations

• I want to be better known in my network (~50% of the surveyed)
• I want to be a thought leader (~38% of the surveyed)
• I want to help others (~43% of the surveyed)

HOW MIGHT WE

• Make novice contributors aware of the different things they can share so that they can be better known in their network
• Help novice contributors get started and feel more confident about sharing their perspective so that they can become thought leaders and help others
• Give novice contributors extrinsic motivations to share

SOLUTION

The final solution is a two part assistive solution that helps users contribute to their network in big ways and small.
• Big contribution: Writing article and sharing perspective
• Small Contribution: Sharing smaller things with the network like a pro-tip, a question or about attending a conference

Solution focuses on supporting novice contributors on their journey to sharing by -
• Nudging them to start
• Assisting them at each step
• Rewarding them for their efforts and contribution.

Assistive publishing tool for big contributions

Assistive share box for small contributions

Assistive share box to make users aware of different things they can share

For a novice contributor who is motivated to contribute but does not know what they should be posting seeing options upfront gives them a nice little nudge on what all is possible and acceptable.

The options I chose to put here are what I gathered from analysing my personal LinkedIn feed and learning from survey.

Assistive publishing tool to make people feel more confident about writing articles

While writing their perspective is the biggest way that novice contributors can be better known in their network and become thought leaders, it is also for them the most daunting since they don’t know what they can write about. What people share is what people think about them and it professional settings the stakes are higher.

So, an assistive tool that-
• Helps them pick topics that are appropriate and acceptable
• Gives them examples of headlines they can write about
• Shows them tips and inspiring articles when they face creative block
• Makes it easier to get early feedback from friends and colleagues
will give them the assurance and the headstart that they are missing today.

DESIGN & RATIONALES

Sharebox Designs

Default State

I decided to consolidate both the upload functions of image and video to consolidate into one CTA. Since I am proposing on introducing discrete CTAs on sharing based on usecases; I decided to bring the most important one upfront and have some sort of a overflow(three dots) CTA to access the rest. The overflow CTA does the same job as if you clicked on the box itself. It brings more visibility to the features upfront. The post button only appears once you have an active text box.

Feature announcement

It is important for the users to know about the new features and it’s capabilities. I used LinkedIn’s current tool-tip pattern to introduce the feature to the user.

Discrete call to actions for possible things to share

Currently, the share box prompts users to share article/photo/video or an idea. There is much more that people are sharing today. Bringing these different things upfront will give direction to the novice contributors and assist them in getting past the feeling of not
knowing what to post.

Also, on web, with the lightbox, I am trying to provide a more immersive experience to the user. Clicking outside the share box will bring it back to normal state.

Contextual prompts

Once users choose what they want to share, they are provided with contextual prompts which nudges them further. For example - if they want to ask a question they are further asked “What question do you want to ask your network?” or if they want to share that they are attending a conference they are further prompted to tell the network about their event and ask who they are attending the conference with. This again directs the user on what all is appropriate and acceptable to post.

Distinguishable look of the post

Different look of these different kind of post - stating what they are about upfront makes it easier for user’s network to understand the purpose of the post.

PRE-ENGRAVED KEYPADS FOR HOMEOWNERS’ MOST FREQUENT ACTIVITIES

We learned in our research how daily activities are a quintessential part of Homeowner’s life. With RA2 Select, we created pre-engraved Pico keypads that have buttons of homeowners most frequent activities.
For example, the Home/Away keypad can be placed at the entry of the home. When the Homeowners is leaving, they can simply tap the away button to turn off all the lights. The Homeowner can relate to these activities, which makes selling easier for the Contractor.
